My work this week was almost exclusively towards the construction of the physical box for our chessboard. I spent almost all of my time this week in the laser cutting lab figuring out how to use the machinery and designing the layout of the board itself. Fortunatly, the base of the chessboard is the size of the wood sheets I bought, so I did not have to do any cutting for those pieces. Due to the stength of the wood, I felt that doubling up on two pieces of wood for the top and bottom pices would help with the structual integrity of the box. The top of the board, has two pieces, both are 240x300 mm in size. The first and top layer, fits arround the LED Matrix, while the second layer is for the board to sit on top of and to be screwed to. The size of the hole for the top layer is 192x192 mm, and the size of the whole of the second layer is 172x172 mm.


The top piece of the chessboard

For the sides of the board, becuase we want the faces towrads the player to be at an angle, they needed to be trapezoids. These pieces were 300 mm in lower width, 60 mm in height, and 240 mm upper width. These pieces I cut using a band saw. Because, of the sizes of the boards, I found I easier to use the natural edge of the board to use as the base of the trapezoids. This allowed me to only make three cut lines rather than four.


The trapezoidal side of the chessboard

I found I could use the laser cutter to shave parts of the wood off, so I put circles around the buttons that were slightly engraved so that the button could be pressed while the wood is below the buttons.

Week 4:

Date: 2-3-2023
Total hours: 7
Description of project design efforts:
This week my work focused on three main areas: microphone input circuitry, learning how to use KiCad, and the Component Analysis document. The Component Analysis can be found on our group website under the Documents tab. Personnally, I completed the sections with regards to Analog to Digital Converters and Microcontrollers of the analysis document. The main progress I made in specific regards to the project focus on the microphone inputs and KiCad tutorials.

For the microphone input, I learned how we would implement a circuit such that we would have filtering and amplification on our microphone input. The filter and gain are very necessary for our microphone input. Without the filtering, the microphone would pick up far too many signals that are out of the frequency range that we desire to pick up. The average range for a human speaking is anywhere from 80 to 255 Hz. Without the filters, many more frequencies would be recorded and could interfere with voice recognition. The gain is simply there to increase the input signal from the microphone, as the analog output made from the microphone itself is fairly low. The circuitry for the filter had a recommended circuit in the datasheet for the MCP3008. Later in the week, our team changed a few of our PSSCs. Originally the microphone input to the amplifier, to the filter, to the ADC, to the Jetson Nano was going to be a PSSC. However, we have changed it to be a stretch goal and replaced it with an SPI output to LCD screens. While this does not mean the research done, and resources purchased are for nought, instead we hope to make this type of microphone a stretch goal, so that we can focus on other aspects of the project.

This week I focused a lot on finding information on microphone inputs and Analog to Digital converters (ADC). In my research, its seems that I have found integrated chips that we could use for an external ADC that outputs to an SPI protocol. The component I found is a MCP3008 - 8-Channel 10-Bit ADC with SPI Interface (pictured below). I spent a couple hour reading the documentation and figuring out any additional circuitry that may be neccasary to support the MCP3008. The datasheets provides clear requirements that are neccasary to use the MCP3008, for a variety of purposes. Due to the functionality of the MCP3008 being able to have 8 input channels, this led to some group conversation about including two microphones for our design functionality, rather than a single microphone input. In addition to finding this component, I also found an additional Operational Amplifier that the maker of the MCP3008 suggests to use with the ADC. This particular OpAmp is the MCP601.

Another element that I learned was for the microphone input itself. If we choose to use a 3.5 mm jack (for user ease of use and compatibility), then we will need to use either a TRRS (tip, ring, ring, sleeve) or TRS (tip, ring, sleeve) jack. The TRS jack would be used with 3.5mm jacks microphones that dont have any audio out, but if we need to use anything that has speakers a TRRS jack will be neccasary. I believe, we are leaning towards the TRRS due to many microphones that a user may want to use could also have a speaker, making TRS not suitable for our needs or the needs of the user.
